About Bornean Pygmy Elephant

The Bornean Pygmy Elephant (Elephas maximus borneensis) is the smallest subspecies of the Asian Elephant, found exclusively in the lowland forests of Sabah and a small area of north Kalimantan, Indonesia. Despite the word "pygmy," adults still stand up to 2.5 metres tall — they are simply smaller and rounder than their mainland Asian counterparts, with notably larger ears and longer tails.

Approximately 1,500 individuals remain in the wild, making it one of the rarest large mammals in Asia. Genetic studies suggest the Bornean Pygmy Elephant has been isolated from other Asian elephant populations for at least 300,000 years, effectively making it a unique and irreplaceable species.

These elephants are highly social animals that live in family groups led by a matriarch. They are known for their gentle, docile temperament compared to other elephant subspecies, and have become a symbol of Sabah's biodiversity and conservation efforts.

Threats & Challenges
🌴

Palm Oil Expansion

The rapid expansion of oil palm plantations in Sabah has destroyed and fragmented vast areas of lowland forest, which represents the Pygmy Elephant's primary habitat and food source.

⚡

Human-Elephant Conflict

As elephants lose their forest habitat, they increasingly raid crops and plantations, leading to retaliation killings and injuries on both sides of the conflict.

🛣️

Infrastructure Development

Roads, dams, and other infrastructure projects cut through elephant movement corridors, isolating herds and preventing natural breeding between populations.

☠️

Poisoning

Some plantation workers and farmers resort to poisoning water sources and salt licks to deter elephants from raiding crops, resulting in deaths and injuries.

🛡️
Conservation Efforts
🌉

Wildlife Corridors

The Sabah Wildlife Department has established wildlife corridors connecting the Lower Kinabatangan Wildlife Sanctuary and Deramakot Forest Reserve to allow safe elephant movement.

📡

GPS Collar Tracking

Individual elephants are fitted with GPS collars to monitor their movements, predict potential conflict zones, and enable rapid response when herds approach settlements.

🚁

Early Warning Systems

Communities near elephant corridors receive SMS alerts when tracked elephants approach, reducing surprise encounters and allowing farmers to protect their crops non-lethally.

🌿

Habitat Restoration

WWF-Malaysia and the Sabah Forestry Department are working to restore degraded forest areas within the Lower Kinabatangan floodplain as part of the Heart of Borneo initiative.
