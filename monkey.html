About Proboscis Monkey

The Proboscis Monkey (Nasalis larvatus) is one of the most distinctive primates in the world, instantly recognisable by the male's extraordinarily large, pendulous nose that can grow up to 10 centimetres long. Endemic to the island of Borneo, it is found in Sabah and Sarawak in Malaysian Borneo, as well as Brunei and Indonesian Kalimantan.

Proboscis Monkeys are strongly associated with coastal and riverine habitats — mangrove forests, riparian forests along rivers, and peat swamp forests. They are excellent swimmers and have been observed crossing rivers by swimming or leaping from overhanging branches, aided by their partially webbed feet.

The species is classified as Endangered by the IUCN, with an estimated population of around 7,000 individuals remaining. Their numbers have declined by over 50% in the last 40 years, driven primarily by the rapid destruction of coastal and riverine forests in Borneo.

⚠️
Threats & Challenges
🌴

Mangrove Destruction

Mangrove forests — the primary habitat of Proboscis Monkeys — are being rapidly cleared for aquaculture, agriculture, and coastal development, eliminating critical feeding and roosting areas.

🌊

Riverine Development

Riverside forest clearing for plantations and settlements removes the riverine forest corridors that Proboscis Monkeys depend on for movement between habitat patches.

🔫

Hunting

Despite legal protection, Proboscis Monkeys are still hunted for bushmeat and captured for the illegal exotic pet trade, particularly in Indonesian Kalimantan.

🏙️

Urban Expansion

Rapid urbanisation along Sabah's coastal areas continues to encroach on mangrove and lowland forest habitats critical to the species' survival.

🛡️
Conservation Efforts
🏞️

Kinabatangan Wildlife Sanctuary

The Lower Kinabatangan Wildlife Sanctuary in Sabah provides protected habitat for Proboscis Monkeys and supports ongoing population monitoring.

🌿

Mangrove Rehabilitation

NGOs and government agencies are working to restore degraded mangrove areas in Sabah and Sarawak, providing essential habitat for the species.

📚

Research & Monitoring

Long-term population studies track Proboscis Monkey groups in key habitats, providing data to inform habitat protection strategies and measure conservation outcomes.

🤝

Community-Based Conservation

Local communities in riverine villages are engaged as conservation partners, providing alternative livelihoods through ecotourism that make forest protection economically viable.
