Proboscis Monkey Population Stabilises Along Lower Kinabatangan — A Conservation Win for Borneo

A Proboscis Monkey group rests along the banks of the Kinabatangan River in Sabah — one of their last remaining strongholds.

A multi-year population monitoring study has revealed that Proboscis Monkey populations along the Lower Kinabatangan River in Sabah have stabilised after decades of decline, with researchers attributing the positive trend to improved habitat protection, community conservation initiatives, and a reduction in hunting pressure in key riparian forest areas.

Decades of Decline — Then a Turning Point

The Proboscis Monkey — recognisable by the male's enormous, pendulous nose — has long been one of Borneo's most iconic and threatened primates. Endemic to the island, the species has suffered dramatic habitat loss as the coastal mangrove forests and riverine habitats it depends on have been cleared for aquaculture, palm oil cultivation, and urban expansion.

The Lower Kinabatangan Wildlife Sanctuary, established in 2005, has been one of the critical lifelines for the species in Sabah. The latest population survey, conducted over four years by a team from Universiti Malaysia Sabah in partnership with the Sabah Wildlife Department and WWF-Malaysia, counted 1,847 individuals across 87 social groups within the sanctuary — a figure consistent with surveys from 2020 and 2022, suggesting the population has reached a point of stability.

"Stability, after decades of documented decline, is itself a victory. It tells us that the conservation investments made in this landscape are working — and that we need to do more of the same."

— Dr. Daicus Belabut, Universiti Malaysia Sabah

Community Conservation as the Cornerstone

Researchers point to community-based conservation as a pivotal factor in the recovery. Villages along the Kinabatangan River have increasingly embraced ecotourism as a primary livelihood — and with tourists paying to observe Proboscis Monkeys from river boats, local communities now have a direct financial stake in the animals' survival.

"When the community earns income from the monkeys being alive, the incentive to protect them becomes very strong," explained Dr. Daicus Belabut, lead researcher from Universiti Malaysia Sabah. "We have seen a significant reduction in hunting incidents in areas where ecotourism is well established — people protect what benefits them."

The Role of Forest Restoration

WWF-Malaysia's Kinabatangan Corridor of Life project has also contributed to the positive results, restoring degraded riparian forest areas along the river's banks and replanting native tree species that provide critical food and roosting habitat for Proboscis Monkeys. To date, more than 500 hectares of riverine forest have been restored within the corridor.

Challenges That Remain

Despite the encouraging results, researchers caution that the stabilisation does not mean the Proboscis Monkey is out of danger. The sanctuary remains an isolated island of habitat in a broader landscape dominated by oil palm plantations, and the monkey population's long-term viability depends on maintaining and expanding forest connectivity. Climate change also poses a growing threat, with changing rainfall patterns affecting the riverine forests that are central to the species' survival.

"Stability is a victory," said Dr. Daicus Belabut. "But we need growth. We need the population to increase, the habitat to expand, and the threats to continue to decrease. This result gives us the confidence that the approach is working — now we need to scale it up."
