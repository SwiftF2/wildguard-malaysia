MYCAT Launches Expanded Tiger Guardian Volunteer Programme — 500 New Recruits Needed

MYCAT Tiger Guardian volunteers preparing for a patrol in a key tiger habitat zone in Peninsular Malaysia.

The Malaysian Conservation Alliance for Tigers (MYCAT) has launched its most ambitious expansion of the Tiger Guardian volunteer programme to date, calling for 500 new volunteers to join anti-poaching patrols, snare removal operations, and wildlife monitoring efforts across key Malayan Tiger habitats in Peninsular Malaysia.

What is the Tiger Guardian Programme?

The Tiger Guardian programme, first launched by MYCAT in 2010, trains members of the public — from university students to working professionals and retirees — to assist trained wildlife rangers in conducting patrol operations in tiger habitats. Volunteers are equipped with field skills including snare identification and removal, wildlife sign tracking, GPS mapping, and camera trap deployment.

"Tiger Guardians are the eyes and ears of conservation in the field," said Tiah Tiang Sing, MYCAT's programme coordinator. "They do not replace professional rangers — but they significantly increase the coverage and frequency of patrols in areas where ranger capacity alone is insufficient to cover the ground." Since its inception, the programme has removed more than 15,000 snares from tiger habitat zones across Peninsular Malaysia.

Why 500 New Volunteers?

The expanded recruitment target reflects both the growing urgency of the tiger conservation situation and the success of the programme's established methodology. MYCAT has identified 12 priority patrol zones across five states — Perak, Kelantan, Pahang, Terengganu, and Johor — where increased volunteer coverage is most critically needed.

Training and Commitment Required

New Tiger Guardian recruits undergo a comprehensive two-day training programme covering field safety, snare identification, wildlife sign interpretation, responsible wildlife observation, and the use of GPS and data collection tools. Following initial training, Guardians commit to a minimum of two patrol operations per year — typically conducted over weekends — in their assigned patrol zone.

MYCAT emphasises that the programme is designed to be accessible to people from all walks of life and does not require any prior wildlife or outdoor experience. "We want teachers, accountants, engineers, students — anyone who cares about tigers and wants to do something tangible about it," said Tiah Tiang Sing. "The only requirement is commitment and a willingness to learn."

How to Sign Up

Malaysians interested in joining the Tiger Guardian programme can register their interest through the MYCAT website at www.mycat.my/tiger-guardian. Applications are open to Malaysian citizens and permanent residents aged 18 and above. MYCAT notes that demand for places has historically exceeded availability, and encourages interested parties to register early.

For those unable to participate in field patrols, MYCAT also operates a Cat Walk fundraising programme and a citizen science initiative through which members of the public can contribute to tiger conservation through online wildlife data review and community awareness activities.
