Sabah Wildlife Department Expands Pygmy Elephant Corridor to Protect Borneo's Gentle Giants

A herd of Bornean Pygmy Elephants making their way through the Lower Kinabatangan floodplain in Sabah.

The Sabah Wildlife Department has announced the establishment of a new 80-kilometre wildlife corridor connecting the Lower Kinabatangan Wildlife Sanctuary with the Deramakot Forest Reserve, providing Bornean Pygmy Elephants with a safe passageway through landscapes that have been increasingly fragmented by palm oil plantations and human settlement.

A Lifeline Through a Fragmented Landscape

The Bornean Pygmy Elephant, found only in Sabah and a small corner of north Kalimantan, has long struggled to navigate a landscape that has been dramatically altered by decades of agricultural expansion. Oil palm plantations now cover vast swathes of what was once contiguous lowland forest, forcing elephants into increasingly dangerous interactions with humans as they search for food and movement routes.

The new corridor, which traverses a mix of protected forest reserves, community forests, and sustainably managed timber concessions, aims to reconnect two of the most important remaining elephant habitats in Sabah. "This corridor is not just a strip of trees," explained Datuk Dr. Laurentius Ambu, Director of the Sabah Wildlife Department. "It is a lifeline — a safe passage that allows elephant herds to move, to find mates, to access food sources, and to behave as wild elephants should."

GPS Tracking Informs Corridor Design

The corridor's design was informed by years of GPS tracking data from collared Pygmy Elephants, which revealed the natural movement routes that herds have historically used — and continue to attempt — despite the barriers posed by plantations and roads. "The elephants showed us where the corridor needed to be," said Dr. Nurzhafarina Othman from the Danau Girang Field Centre. "Our job was to work with landowners and the government to make those routes safe and passable."

Reducing Human-Elephant Conflict

Human-elephant conflict remains one of the most significant challenges facing Pygmy Elephant conservation. As elephants venture into plantations and farmland in search of food, they destroy crops and property, leading to retaliatory actions by affected communities — ranging from harassment to deliberate poisoning. The new corridor aims to reduce these encounters by providing elephants with sufficient natural habitat and food sources to reduce the need to venture into agricultural areas.

The project is also implementing an early warning system using GPS collar data to alert communities when tracked elephant herds approach settlements, giving farmers time to protect their crops using non-lethal deterrents such as chilli-smoke barriers and beehive fences.

A Model for Conservation in Human-Modified Landscapes

The Sabah Pygmy Elephant corridor is being watched closely by conservationists across Southeast Asia as a potential model for wildlife conservation in landscapes dominated by agriculture and human settlement. "We cannot simply draw lines on a map and call them protected areas," said WWF-Malaysia's Chief Executive Dr. Sophia Lim. "We need to work with the communities and industries that share the landscape with wildlife, and find solutions that work for everyone."
