Malaysia Strengthens Wildlife Protection Laws with Harsher Penalties for Poachers

A Malayan Tapir photographed in a forest reserve in Peninsular Malaysia — one of the species that will benefit from strengthened wildlife legislation.

The Malaysian government has passed sweeping amendments to the Wildlife Conservation Act 2010, introducing dramatically harsher penalties for wildlife crimes including poaching, wildlife trafficking, and the illegal keeping of protected species — in what conservationists are calling the most significant strengthening of wildlife law in Malaysia's history.

Key Changes to the Law

Under the amended Wildlife Conservation Act 2026, the maximum fine for poaching a totally protected species — such as the Malayan Tiger, Sumatran Rhinoceros, or Bornean Pygmy Elephant — has been increased from RM100,000 to RM500,000 per offence, with a mandatory minimum prison sentence of five years, up from the previous maximum of three years. Repeat offenders face a minimum of ten years imprisonment and fines of up to RM1 million.

The amendments also introduce new offences for the possession and trafficking of wildlife parts and derivatives — including animal skins, bones, scales, and horns — with equivalent penalties to those for the killing of the animal itself. "For too long, traffickers have operated in the shadows, knowing that the financial rewards far outweighed the legal risks," said Minister of Energy and Natural Resources Datuk Seri Nik Nazmi Nik Ahmad. "These amendments change that calculation fundamentally."

Conservation Community Responds

Conservation organisations across Malaysia and internationally have welcomed the amendments, though many emphasise that strong legislation must be backed by robust enforcement to have real-world impact. "Laws on paper only work if they are enforced on the ground," said WWF-Malaysia's Chief Executive Dr. Sophia Lim. "We welcome these amendments wholeheartedly, and we call on the government to ensure that PERHILITAN has the resources, training, and personnel necessary to implement them effectively."

TRAFFIC Southeast Asia, which monitors the illegal wildlife trade in the region, noted that Malaysia has long been both a source country and a transit hub for illegally traded wildlife products. Stricter penalties, combined with improved intelligence sharing with neighbouring countries, could significantly disrupt trafficking networks that currently operate with relative impunity across porous borders.

A New Court Specialising in Wildlife Crime

Alongside the legislative amendments, the Malaysian judiciary has announced the establishment of a dedicated Wildlife Crime Court within the Sessions Court system, with specially trained magistrates who will handle all wildlife crime cases. The move is designed to ensure more consistent sentencing and faster case resolution — addressing a long-standing concern that wildlife crime cases were handled too slowly and inconsistently within the general court system.

What This Means for Malaysia's Wildlife

For species like the Malayan Tiger, Sumatran Rhinoceros, and Helmeted Hornbill — all of which have been heavily impacted by poaching and illegal trade — the strengthened legislation represents a critical line of defence. "Strong laws are one of the pillars of effective conservation," said MYCAT's programme coordinator. "Combined with habitat protection, community engagement, and public education, this gives us a real fighting chance for these species."
