About Malayan Tiger

The Malayan Tiger (Panthera tigris jacksoni) is a tiger subspecies found exclusively in the forests of Peninsular Malaysia. It is Malaysia's national symbol, proudly featured on the country's coat of arms, and deeply embedded in Malaysian cultural identity.

With fewer than 150 individuals estimated to remain in the wild, the Malayan Tiger is classified as Critically Endangered by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). It is one of the rarest big cats on Earth, and without urgent intervention, it faces extinction within our lifetimes.

Malayan Tigers are solitary, territorial animals that require large, undisturbed forest areas to thrive. They are apex predators, playing a vital role in maintaining the balance of their ecosystem by regulating prey populations such as deer and wild boar.

⚠️
Threats & Challenges
🌲

Habitat Loss & Fragmentation

Large-scale deforestation for palm oil plantations, logging, and agricultural expansion has destroyed and fragmented vast areas of tiger habitat, isolating populations and reducing prey availability.

🔫

Poaching

Tigers are illegally poached for their skins, bones, and organs, which are highly valued in illegal wildlife markets and traditional medicine industries across Asia.

🪤

Snaring

Indiscriminate wire snares set by poachers targeting other wildlife frequently injure and kill tigers, representing one of the most immediate threats to the species.

🚗

Human-Wildlife Conflict

As forests shrink, tigers increasingly come into contact with human settlements, leading to conflict, retaliatory killings, and further population decline.

🛡️
Conservation Efforts
🛡️

MYCAT Tiger Guardian Programme

MYCAT trains community volunteers to conduct anti-poaching patrols and remove snares from key tiger habitats across Peninsular Malaysia.

📷

Camera Trap Monitoring

PERHILITAN and WWF-Malaysia operate extensive camera trap networks to monitor tiger populations, track individual animals, and detect poaching activity.

🌿

Habitat Corridor Protection

The Royal Belum-Temengor forest complex and the Central Forest Spine initiative aim to connect fragmented forest patches, allowing tigers to roam and breed across a wider area.

⚖️

Strengthened Legislation

Malaysia's Wildlife Conservation Act 2010 (Amendment 2026) imposes harsher penalties for tiger poaching, with fines up to RM500,000 and mandatory imprisonment.
