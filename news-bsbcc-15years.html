Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre Celebrates 15 Years — Over 20 Bears Returned to the Wild

A rehabilitated sun bear explores the forest at the Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre's forest enclosure in Sandakan, Sabah.

The Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre (BSBCC) in Sandakan, Sabah, is marking its 15th anniversary this year — a milestone that celebrates not only 15 years of tireless dedication to the world's smallest bear, but also the successful rehabilitation and release of over 20 sun bears back into protected forest, and the rescue of more than 200 bears from illegal captivity.

From a Single Bear to a Global Movement

The BSBCC was founded in 2008 by wildlife biologist Wong Siew Te, who had spent years studying sun bears in the wild and growing increasingly alarmed by the scale of their exploitation and the lack of any dedicated facility to care for rescued individuals. The centre began with a handful of bears confiscated from illegal captivity — kept as pets, or held in horrific conditions for bile farming — and a small team of dedicated staff operating on a shoestring budget.

"When we started, there was nowhere in the world that specialised in sun bear care," recalled Wong Siew Te. "We were learning as we went — learning how to feed them, how to rehabilitate them, how to prepare them for a return to the wild. Every bear taught us something new." Today, the BSBCC is recognised globally as the world leader in sun bear research, rescue, and rehabilitation.

A Second Chance for Over 200 Bears

Over the past 15 years, BSBCC has received more than 200 sun bears — individuals confiscated from the illegal pet trade, rescued from bile farms, orphaned due to habitat destruction, or injured by snares and human activity. Each bear undergoes a carefully managed rehabilitation programme, starting with veterinary treatment and progressing through socialisation, foraging training, and extended access to the centre's 8-hectare natural forest enclosure.

More than 20 bears have been deemed fit for release and returned to protected forest areas in Sabah, where they are monitored using radio telemetry to assess their survival and adaptation. "Every release is both a celebration and a moment of anxiety," said centre manager Thye Lim. "We have done everything we can to prepare them — but the forest is wild, and there are no guarantees."

"Every bear taught us something new. When we started, there was nowhere in the world that specialised in sun bear care. Now we are the global reference point for this species."

— Wong Siew Te, Founder, Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre

Research That Shapes Global Policy

Beyond rescue and rehabilitation, BSBCC has produced groundbreaking research on sun bear ecology, behaviour, and health that has directly influenced conservation policy in Malaysia and internationally. Studies conducted at the centre have revealed previously unknown aspects of sun bear social behaviour, dietary preferences, and the long-term psychological impacts of captivity — findings that have improved care standards for sun bears in facilities worldwide.

The Next 15 Years

As BSBCC looks to its future, the team is focused on expanding its education and community outreach programmes, increasing the scale of its release efforts, and advocating for stronger legal protections for sun bears across Southeast Asia. "The next 15 years need to be about scale," said Wong Siew Te. "We have proven that rehabilitation works. Now we need to ensure that the forests these bears are released into are protected for generations to come."
